## Powers

**Gravitational force manipulation** — result of the 2020 Glass District gravity core collapse. A.E.G.I.S. stabilized the anomaly inside him; what remained is a persistent, integrated gravitational field he can modulate with precision.

- **Immobilization** — selectively increase gravitational force on a target or area
- **Momentum nullification** — arrest the kinetic energy of moving objects or individuals
- **Compression shielding** — dense gravitational field around himself or others; functions as near-impenetrable defense against physical force
- **Vector perception** — passive awareness of directional force across a radius; hard to approach without him knowing

Darius is among the most defensively capable members of the extended Alliance network. A.E.G.I.S. built him as a symbol as much as an asset.

---

## Background

Marine Corps officer. Decorated service record. Was in the Glass District in 2020 for reasons that are either routine or classified depending on who you ask. The gravity core event was documented as an equipment malfunction. Darius was the only person in the event radius who survived, and he survived because A.E.G.I.S. moved fast.

The procedure that stabilized him was not exactly documented as optional. He was given a choice in the way people are given choices when the alternative is death. He said yes. He has been saying yes to A.E.G.I.S. in various forms since.

**Aegis Prime the symbol:** A.E.G.I.S. needed something it could point to when the public asked whether the agency had its own heroes or just oversaw other people's. Darius became that thing. He understands this. He is not naive about what he is to the institution. He also genuinely believes what he believes: that power needs structure, that heroes without accountability are a liability, that someone has to hold the line.

The fact that A.E.G.I.S. benefits from him believing this does not make the belief false.

---

## Character Profile

Darius is not a villain. He is not a pawn who doesn't know he's being used. He is a man with a genuine ideology that happens to align neatly with institutional interests, and he has thought about whether that's a coincidence and concluded — for now — that it isn't.

He believes in accountability. He thinks the Alliance's independence is a structural vulnerability, not a virtue. He thinks Riley in particular operates with a level of personal latitude that will eventually cause something unfixable. He is not wrong about all of this.

His friction with Alliance members is ideological, not personal. He does not dislike them. He thinks they are making a mistake that will cost someone something, and he thinks they won't recognize the cost until it's already paid.
